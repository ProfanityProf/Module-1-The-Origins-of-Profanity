Section 1 — Introduction

Why study profanity?

Before You Read

Before you go any further: what do you actually think profanity is? Write down a definition in your own words. Don't overthink it. We'll come back to test it against the research by the time you finish this module.

Profanity is easy to recognize and surprisingly hard to define. You know it when you hear it — but pinning down exactly what makes a word profane turns out to be a genuinely complex question that touches linguistics, history, psychology, law, and culture all at once.

This course starts with a simple premise: profanity is not just rude language. It is a window. Through it, we can see what a society fears, what it values, who holds power, and what happens when that power is challenged. Steven Pinker describes swear words as “kidnappers of attention” — language that hijacks the listener's emotional system regardless of their will (Pinker, 2007, p. 329). That involuntary power is worth taking seriously.

Module 1 builds the analytical foundation for everything that follows. By the end, you should be able to define profanity across multiple frameworks, explain where it comes from physiologically, historically, and linguistically, and identify the social forces that determine which words end up on the forbidden list.

Section 2 — What Is It?

Defining the
undefinable

Here is the first problem: there is no single, agreed-upon definition of profanity. Different disciplines define it differently, and each definition reflects the values and concerns of the framework it comes from. That's not a bug — it's the first important analytical insight.

▼ Popular definition — Eldridge / Collins

“Language that is considered socially offensive due to being vulgar, obscene, or irreverent.” (Eldridge, Britannica)

Useful as a starting point, but circular — it defines profanity as language people find offensive, which doesn't explain why they find it offensive or how that judgment varies across contexts.

▼ Religious / historical definition — OED / Merriam-Webster

“The quality or state of being profane; irreverence for or contempt of sacred things; sacrilege.” (OED)

The word “profane” itself comes from the Latin pro fanum — literally, “before the temple” or “outside the sacred.” Historically, profanity meant language that violated the boundary between the sacred and the secular. This origin explains why blasphemy and religious oaths dominated early definitions of “bad language.”

▼ Linguistic definition — Allan & Burridge / Jay

Allan and Burridge define profanity as language that “desecrates or shows lack of respect for religion, deities, or holy things; in modern use, it often overlaps with swearwords or general bad language” (2006, p. 1).

Timothy Jay extends this further: profanity is “a subcategory of cursing in which language is used to offend or desecrate” but has broadened over time to include taboo expressions not directly tied to religion (2009, p. 153). The linguistic definition is important because it emphasizes the function of profanity — what it does rather than just what it is.

▼ Legal definition — FCC

In U.S. broadcast law, profanity is defined as “language so grossly offensive to members of the public who actually hear it as to amount to a nuisance.” (FCC)

Notice that this definition is effect-based — the word isn't inherently profane, it's profane when and because it offends. It also applies only to broadcast contexts. There is no equivalent federal regulation of spoken language in Canada. Legal definitions reveal what governments are willing to enforce, which is its own kind of statement about power.

▼ Cultural definition — Pinker

Pinker situates profanity within the larger system of “swearing,” which includes curses, oaths, insults, and taboo words. Profanity “specifically involves language that violates norms of respect, especially toward the sacred or the socially sensitive” (2007, p. 325).

The cultural definition is the most flexible — and the most honest about the fact that what counts as “profane” is not fixed but socially constructed and historically variable.

The vocabulary around profanity

People use a cluster of related terms somewhat interchangeably in everyday speech. Analytically, they mean different things. Here are the key distinctions:

▼ Profanity

Language that violates norms of respect toward the sacred or the socially sensitive. The broadest and most commonly used umbrella term.

▼ Swearing / Cursing

Interchangeable in common use. Technically, “swearing” historically referred to oath-taking; “cursing” to invoking harm on someone. Both now broadly mean using taboo language.

▼ Slurs

Words or expressions used to insult, dehumanize, or marginalize people based on identity. Slurs target groups and carry historical weight of oppression. Distinct from other profanity in their potential for harm.

▼ Blasphemy

Speech that shows contempt or irreverence toward a deity, religion, or sacred thing. Historically the primary category of forbidden language; legally actionable in some jurisdictions.

▼ Obscenity

Language (or content) that is sexually explicit or grossly offensive. Obscenity has a specific legal meaning in the U.S. and carries particular weight in broadcast regulation.

▼ Expletive

A word or phrase used to fill space or express strong emotion, without contributing lexical meaning. Expletives are often profane, but not necessarily: “oh well” is technically an expletive.

▼ Epithet

A descriptive word or phrase expressing a quality. Can be neutral (“Alexander the Great”) or deeply derogatory when used to reduce a person to a single characteristic, often tied to identity.

▼ Vulgarity

Language considered crude, unrefined, or indecent. More about register and class connotation than strictly about offensiveness — “vulgar” often signals low social status as much as transgression.

Taboo: the concept underneath profanity

The word taboo comes from the Polynesian word tapu, meaning something spiritual but restricted — simultaneously sacred and forbidden. It was introduced into English by Captain James Cook after his 1771 expedition to Tonga, and it has stuck because it captures something precise: a category of thing that is charged, powerful, and off-limits all at once.

Taboo language is not just offensive language. It is language that carries a special charge — language that activates an emotional and physiological response that ordinary words do not. That charge is precisely why profanity is worth studying: it reveals what a culture has invested with fear, awe, or power.

Context Matters

Consider: “Hey little fella — do you need to take a shit?” Spoken by a veterinarian to a dog in a clinical context, this sentence is unremarkable. Spoken to a person in a professional setting, it becomes offensive. The word didn't change — the context did.

This is the most fundamental insight about profanity: no word is inherently profane. Profanity is not a property of words. It is a property of the relationship between words, speakers, audiences, and social contexts. This is why the study of profanity is really the study of social norms.

Taboo avoidance strategies

Because profanity carries charge, human beings have developed creative ways to approximate it without fully crossing the line. The two main strategies:

Euphemism / Minced Oath

Replacing a taboo word with a softened variation that preserves the emotional register without the full transgression. “Sugar!” for “shit,” “fudge” for “f*ck,” “dang” for “damn.”

The interesting thing: the speaker and listener both know exactly what is being approximated. The avoidance is partly real, partly performance.

Grawlixes (Obscenicons)

The typographical substitution of symbols for profanity: @#$%! This convention originated in early newspaper comics and was formalized by cartoonist Mort Walker. The term “grawlix” is his invention.

Like the minced oath, grawlixes perform censorship while simultaneously signaling that something profane is happening. The absence marks the presence.

The use-mention distinction

One of the most analytically important concepts in the study of profanity is the distinction between using a word and mentioning it.

Use

Deploying a word to do its ordinary work in speech.

“Get out of my way, you a**hole.”
The word is being aimed at someone as an insult.

Mention

Referring to a word as an object of analysis or discussion.

“The word ‘a**hole’ is used as both a particularist insult and a general pejorative.”
The word is being examined, not deployed.

This distinction matters enormously in academic and legal contexts. A linguist writing about slurs is mentioning them. Someone directing them at a person is using them. The moral weight is entirely different. In this course, we operate primarily in the “mention” register — we analyze language as an object of study, not as a weapon or an endorsement.

Section 3 — Where Does It Come From?

Mohr's three lenses:
physiology, history, language

Melissa Mohr, in Holy Shit: A Brief History of Swearing (2013), offers a framework for understanding profanity from three complementary angles. None of these lenses is sufficient on its own — together they explain why profanity is what it is.

Lens 1

Physiological

Profanity is rooted in the body. Swear words activate the emotional brain — the limbic system — in ways that ordinary words do not. This is not metaphor; it is neuroscience.

Lens 2

Historical / Social

What counts as profane changes across time and culture. A tour through history shows that the content of “bad language” shifts radically — but the social function of having bad language stays constant.

Lens 3

Linguistic

Profanity has distinct linguistic properties — phonetic patterns, grammatical flexibility, memory retention — that set it apart from ordinary vocabulary. Language itself gives profanity special structural features.

Lens 1: Physiological

Here's a simple experiment to demonstrate the physiological dimension of profanity: try to memorize two lists of ten words each. One list contains neutral words (collaborate, book, exhaust, skate, olives). The other contains taboo words (f*ck, a**hole, c*m, b*stard, d*psh*t). Test yourself twenty minutes later. Almost everyone recalls significantly more words from the taboo list.

Why? Because profanity is processed differently in the brain. Ordinary language is handled primarily by the cerebral cortex — the rational, analytical brain. But taboo words activate the limbic system: the older, emotional brain responsible for fear, pleasure, aggression, and memory consolidation. Emotional arousal improves retention. This is not a moral judgment — it is a neurological fact.

This also explains coprolalia — the involuntary vocalization of taboo words or phrases, a symptom associated with some forms of Tourette syndrome. When the cortex loses control of the limbic system momentarily, what comes out is often profanity. The limbic system, it appears, stores swear words in a way that makes them more readily available and harder to suppress.

This physiological grounding is why Pinker calls swear words “kidnappers of attention” — they hijack the listener's emotional system before the rational brain has a chance to intervene (Pinker, 2007, p. 329). That involuntary power is the engine behind most of profanity's social functions.

Lens 2: Historical / Social

Mohr observes that profanity has always occupied what she calls “two spheres of the unsayable” — the sacred (language that violates religious or spiritual taboo) and the obscene (language that violates bodily or sexual taboo). The balance between these two spheres shifts with history.

Antiquity

Sacred sphere dominant. Greeks and Romans invoked gods in curses and oaths. Religious language at sacred sites carried the most charge. Obscenity existed but was considered less serious.

Middle Ages

Blasphemy and sacrilege were the most serious speech crimes. God, the Virgin Mary, and Jesus were the most invoked targets. Taking the Lord's name in vain could carry legal consequences.

Renaissance & Enlightenment

Religious authority declines as a cultural organizer. Focus shifts toward sexuality and bodily functions. “Vulgar” and “obscene” language begin to carry more weight than blasphemy in everyday contexts.

Victorian Era

The obscene sphere intensifies. Bodily functions, body parts, and sexuality become heavily policed. This is the era that produced most of the “classic” English profanity still considered transgressive today.

20th Century

Sexual liberation and countercultural movements make bodily profanity more acceptable. The most charged language shifts toward derogatory terms targeting identity — race, gender, sexuality, disability.

21st Century

Taboo is now centered on respect, dignity, and identity. The worst language you can use is typically language that dehumanizes people based on who they are. What was once the most transgressive (religious blasphemy) is now often unremarkable.

Lens 3: Linguistic

Profanity has structural features that distinguish it from ordinary vocabulary. These are not accidents — they are part of why taboo words are so psychologically potent.

Memory retention

Taboo words are recalled better and faster than neutral words. Emotional arousal during encoding produces stronger memory traces (Jay, 2009).

Grammatical flexibility

Strong profanity can function as noun, verb, adjective, adverb, and interjection — often in the same conversation. This multi-functionality is linguistically unusual.

Sound symbolism

English profanity tends to cluster around hard consonant sounds — particularly stops like /k/, /t/, and /p/. Some researchers argue these sounds are inherently more aggressive-sounding across languages.

“Kidnappers of attention”

Pinker's term captures the linguistic mechanism: profanity bypasses the rational processing pathway and triggers an automatic emotional response. The listener cannot choose not to be affected (Pinker, 2007, p. 329).

Section 4 — Who / What Decides?

The forces that make
words bad

No word arrives pre-loaded with badness. The question “who decides what's profane?” is one of the most important in this course — because the answer is never neutral. The forces that police language also police ideas, identities, and power.

Family

The first language police most people encounter. “We don't say that in this house.” Family norms around profanity vary widely by culture, religion, class, and generation.

Community & Peers

What's acceptable among close friends may be unacceptable at work or with family. Community norms enforce themselves through social approval and disapproval, not law.

Social Institutions

Schools, workplaces, and religious organizations each maintain their own codes. Violating them has real consequences: detention, termination, expulsion.

Pop Culture & Media

Film ratings, music censorship, streaming standards — media industries both police language and drive its evolution. They are simultaneously gatekeepers and agents of change.

Government

The only institution with legal authority to enforce language norms. In the U.S., the FCC regulates broadcast language. In 1978, the Supreme Court upheld restrictions on George Carlin's “Seven Dirty Words” broadcast. No equivalent federal regulation exists in Canada.

Social Norms & Taboos

The most diffuse and pervasive force: collective, unwritten, and enforced through shame and social exclusion. Also the most likely to shift over time as culture changes.

“Bad words create the boundaries of what we reject and accept as a society.”

— Vsauce, Why Are Bad Words Bad? (2013)

This quote is deceptively simple but analytically rich. If bad words define social boundaries, then decisions about which words are bad are also decisions about who and what belongs inside those boundaries — and who doesn't. Language policing is never just about decorum. It is always also about power.

A typology of swearing

Not all profanity is the same. Research identifies several broad categories of taboo language, each drawing on a different cluster of cultural anxieties. Notice which categories are considered most harmful today — and why that might be.

The purple categories are derogatory slurs targeting social identity. These are not just “stronger” profanity — they function differently, carry historical weight of oppression, and cause harm in ways that most other profanity does not. We return to this distinction in Modules 3 and 4.

How words become bad — and sometimes stop being bad

Words don't arrive in the dictionary pre-charged with offense. They acquire it. Two related processes explain how:

Semantic Pejoration

The process through which a word becomes more negative, insulting, or offensive over time because of its sustained association with stigmatized people or things.

Examples: “villain” (once just a farmhand), “hussy” (once just a housewife), “idiot” and “moron” (once clinical diagnostic terms).

Semantic Amelioration

The reverse process: a word loses its charged or negative associations and becomes more neutral or positive. This can happen through gradual drift or through deliberate reclamation by a marginalized community.

Examples: “nice” (once meant foolish), “nerd” (once purely an insult, now often a badge of pride), “queer” (reclaimed as a term of identity and pride).

The word “queer” deserves particular attention as an example of reclamation — the deliberate appropriation of a slur by the community it was used against. Reclamation is a political act, and an important one. But it also has limits: a word reclaimed by one community does not become freely available to everyone. That distinction matters throughout this course.

Whether a word is “bad” tells you everything about the society doing the judging — and almost nothing about the word itself.

Section 5 — Global Lens

Is profanity the
same everywhere?

The frameworks in this module — Mohr's three lenses, the social forces model, semantic pejoration — are drawn primarily from Western, English-language scholarship. They are useful. They are not universal.

Other cultural and linguistic traditions offer frameworks that ask the same questions — what is it, where does it come from, who decides? — and arrive at genuinely different answers. These aren't footnotes or supplements. They are co-equal analytical lenses.

▼ Japanese kotodama: words carry inherent power

The Japanese concept of kotodama holds that words carry inherent spiritual power capable of affecting reality — not just describing it. This is a fundamentally different answer to “where does profanity's power come from?” than the Western social convention model.

Under kotodama, linguistic taboos arise not from social judgment but from the intrinsic nature of language. Numbers that sound like death words (4 sounds like shi) are avoided because they are genuinely dangerous, not just impolite. This framework challenges the assumption that profanity is “just” a social construction.

▼ Arabic and Islamic ethics: a graduated framework

Where Western frameworks tend to sort language into “acceptable” and “profane,” Islamic jurisprudence offers a graduated model: haram (forbidden), makruh (discouraged but not prohibited), and permissible — with classification depending on content, context, intent, and likely social effect.

Notably, many of the most serious speech violations in this tradition — backbiting (ghiba), slander (namima), speech that damages social trust — are not “profane” in the Western sense at all. The ethical weight is placed on relational harm rather than on a catalogue of forbidden words.

▼ Yoruba egan: institutionalized ritual insult

Among the Yoruba of West Africa, egan is a recognized form of ceremonial verbal sparring — structured, sometimes obscene, sometimes insulting — deployed in specific social contexts to resolve tension and reinforce group bonds. It is not transgressive; it is sanctioned.

This challenges the assumption embedded in most Western frameworks that taboo language is by definition a violation of social norms. In egan contexts, the transgression is the norm. The framework for what counts as “bad language” is not universal — it is contextual and culturally specific.

▼ Indigenous North American traditions: relational and sacred speech

Many Indigenous linguistic traditions in North America do not share the Western category of “profanity” at all. What constitutes harmful or sacred speech is defined relationally — by the connection between speaker, listener, land, community, and the living world. Language is understood as embedded in responsibility, not as a neutral medium occasionally contaminated by bad words.

The suppression of Indigenous languages in Canadian residential schools was not merely a cultural inconvenience — it was an attack on entire systems of relational knowledge, ceremony, and identity. Language suppression was a deliberate colonial strategy precisely because language is not separable from the community it carries (Truth and Reconciliation Commission of Canada, 2015).

Glossary

Module 1 Key Terms

▼ Profanity

Language that violates norms of respect toward the sacred or socially sensitive. The broadest umbrella term in this field, with definitions that vary across religious, linguistic, legal, popular, and cultural frameworks.

▼ Taboo

From the Polynesian tapu — something simultaneously sacred and forbidden. In linguistics, taboo language is language that carries special charge: it activates emotional and physiological responses that ordinary language does not.

▼ Use-mention distinction

The difference between using a word (deploying it to do its ordinary work, e.g. as an insult) and mentioning it (referring to the word as an object of analysis or discussion). In academic study of profanity, we operate primarily in the “mention” register.

▼ Euphemism / Minced oath

A softened word or phrase used in place of a taboo term. A minced oath is specifically a euphemistic corruption of profanity (e.g. “sugar” for “shit”). Both preserve the emotional register of the original while providing plausible deniability.

▼ Grawlix (Obscenicon)

Typographical symbols (“@#$%!”) substituted for profanity, originating in comic strips. Term coined by cartoonist Mort Walker. Signals the presence of profanity while technically avoiding it.

▼ Limbic system

The part of the brain responsible for emotional processing, memory consolidation, and instinctive responses. Taboo language is processed in the limbic system rather than primarily in the cortex — explaining why profanity has an involuntary emotional charge that ordinary language lacks.

▼ Coprolalia

The involuntary vocalization of obscene or taboo words or phrases. Associated with some forms of Tourette syndrome. Its existence is evidence that profanity is stored and processed differently in the brain than ordinary language.

▼ Mohr's framework

Melissa Mohr's three-lens approach to understanding profanity: the physiological lens (how profanity works in the brain and body), the historical/social lens (how the content of profanity has changed across time and culture), and the linguistic lens (the structural features that make taboo language distinctive).

▼ Semantic pejoration

The process through which a word acquires more negative, offensive, or demeaning connotations over time through sustained association with stigmatized people, conditions, or behaviors.

▼ Semantic amelioration

The reverse of pejoration: a word loses its charged or negative associations and becomes more neutral or positive, either through gradual drift or through deliberate reclamation by a community.

▼ “Kidnappers of attention”

Steven Pinker's phrase for the distinctive linguistic power of profanity: the ability to hijack a listener's emotional and attentional system before the rational brain can intervene. The involuntary response to taboo language — not a moral failing, but a neurological mechanism — is what gives profanity its power (Pinker, 2007, p. 329).
