Section 01

What Is Profanity?

You'd think this would be the easy question. It isn't. "Profanity" means something different depending on who's defining it — and that disagreement turns out to be the whole point.

The word "profanity" derives from the Latin profanus — meaning "before the temple" or "outside the sacred space." Originally, to be profane was to be literally outside the holy. Over time, the meaning shifted to describe language that violates what a community holds sacred, whether that sacredness is religious, social, or both.

Here is the problem: whose sacred space? Whose norms? This is not an abstract philosophical question. It determines whether someone gets fired, suspended, prosecuted, or censored. As linguist Timothy Jay (2009) argues, profanity cannot be meaningfully defined without also specifying the cultural and historical context in which it is being evaluated. A word that is deeply offensive in one community may be entirely unremarkable in another.

"Not merely offensive due to their meaning but considered taboo in and of themselves."
Stephen Eldridge's definition, highlighting the distinction between meaning and status

Five major frameworks for defining profanity:

Religious
Sacred Violation
Profanity as irreverence toward the sacred. Language that desecrates, disrespects, or invokes divine forces improperly. This is the oldest definition, and it shaped English profanity law for centuries.
Oxford English Dictionary; Merriam-Webster
Linguistic
Taboo Language
Language that desecrates or shows disrespect toward religion, deities, or holy things; in modern use it broadly overlaps with swearwords. Any language that violates norms of social decency.
Allan & Burridge (2006); Timothy Jay (2009)
Legal
Public Nuisance
The U.S. Federal Communications Commission (FCC) defines profanity as language "so grossly offensive to members of the public who actually hear it as to amount to a nuisance." Canada has no direct equivalent, leaving considerably more room for ambiguity.
FCC Indecency Guidelines
Popular
Rude Words
Profanity consists of swear words or offensive language. Profanities are rude or offensive words or expressions. Simple and circular — which is part of why popular definitions are inadequate for academic analysis.
Collins English Dictionary
Cultural
Norm Violation
Profanity is part of the larger system of "swearing," which includes curses, oaths, insults, and taboo words. Profanity specifically involves language that violates norms of respect, especially toward the sacred or the socially sensitive.
Pinker, The Stuff of Thought (2007)
Think About It

Notice how each definition above raises its own unanswered question. The religious definition asks: sacred to whom? The legal definition asks: offensive to which members of the public? The popular definition is effectively circular — profanity is offensive language, and offensive language is profanity. What does it tell us that we struggle this much to define a category of words that everyone seems to recognize instantly?

Section 02

The Terminology Field

Profanity doesn't exist alone. It sits inside a cluster of overlapping, related concepts — and precision matters. Using these terms correctly is the foundation of every analysis you'll do in this course.

"Profanity," "swearing," "vulgarity," and "obscenity" are often used interchangeably in everyday speech — but they are analytically distinct. Lumping them together is a bit like calling every citrus fruit a "lemon." The distinctions reveal different social histories, different legal regimes, and different psychological mechanisms.

One place to start: the word TABOO itself. It comes from the Polynesian word tapu, meaning something simultaneously spiritual and forbidden. Captain James Cook brought the word into English in 1777 after his voyages to the Pacific. The concept that some things are sacred and dangerous to touch — whether literally or linguistically — is nearly universal across human cultures, even if the specific content of what is taboo varies dramatically.

Profanity
Language that violates norms around the sacred or socially sensitive; the umbrella category for this course.
Swearing / Swear Words
The broader act of using taboo language, including oaths, curses, and expletives. All profanity can be swearing, but not all swearing is profanity.
Slurs
Words that target people based on identity (race, gender, sexuality, disability, etc.). The most socially consequential category of taboo language.
Cursing / Cussing
Calling for harm or misfortune upon someone through language. The oldest form of "bad language" — invoking supernatural forces against an enemy.
Blasphemy
Specifically religious profanity: language that desecrates or shows contempt for God, sacred figures, or religious institutions. Still a criminal offence in some jurisdictions.
Obscenity
Language (or content) considered offensive to prevailing standards of decency, particularly regarding sex or bodily functions. More often a legal than a linguistic category.
Expletive
A word used to add emphasis or fill space, often with no literal meaning (e.g., "what the hell?"). Expletives are frequently profane but don't have to be.
Epithet
A word or phrase used to characterize someone, often insultingly. Slurs are a subcategory of epithets.
Vulgarity
Language associated with low social status or crude behaviour. More about class and taste than morality.
Euphemism
A mild or indirect substitute for something considered too blunt or offensive. "Passed away" instead of "died." The polite side of taboo avoidance.
Dysphemism
The opposite: using a harsher, more offensive substitute. Makes something sound worse than it is. Calling police "pigs" is dysphemistic.
Grawlix / Obsenicon
The typographic substitution of symbols for swear words: @#$%! Found in comic strips since the early 20th century.

Notice that euphemism and dysphemism are both forms of taboo avoidance — one softens the blow, the other amplifies it. Allan and Burridge (2006) refer to these as complementary strategies in what they call the "lexical toolkit" for navigating socially sensitive topics. The existence of grawlixes (those @#$%! substitutions) tells us something interesting: we can signal that a word is being avoided without actually saying it, and the social effect is largely preserved.

Section 03

Context Matters — and So Does the Use–Mention Distinction

The same word can be perfectly acceptable or deeply offensive depending entirely on who says it, to whom, in what setting, and for what purpose. This isn't wishy-washy relativism — it's a structural feature of how language works.

Consider the word "shit." In a medical context, "feces" is the clinical term and "shit" is taboo. But in many households, parents matter-of-factly ask toddlers whether they need to "take a shit" — the intimate, informal register of family life. The word hasn't changed. The context has. As Pinker (2007) argues, profanity is not about the acoustics of a word; it is about the social relationship between speaker, audience, and setting at the moment of utterance.

One of the most important analytical tools for this course is the use–mention distinction. When we use a word, we deploy it to do its job — to insult, offend, describe. When we mention a word, we are talking about the word itself as an object of study. Philosophers and linguists often mark this with quotation marks: to say "the word 'fuck' has Germanic roots" is to mention the word; to say something aggressively at someone is to use it. This course operates largely in the mention register — we are examining these words, not wielding them.

This distinction is not just academic. It is why researchers, teachers, and journalists can discuss slurs in educational contexts. It is also why the distinction can be weaponized — claiming to "merely mention" a word while using it in ways that cause real harm. The use–mention line is not always clean, and part of critical language analysis is learning to read when that line is being blurred.

Think About It

In 2020, CBC News anchor Wendy Mesley was suspended after she used a racial slur during internal editorial meetings — reportedly while discussing how media should cover stories that reference that word. She argued she was in "mention" mode; others experienced it as use. Where do you think the line falls, and who gets to draw it?

Section 04

Characterizing Profanity — Mohr's Three Lenses

Historian Melissa Mohr argues that to properly understand profanity, you need three overlapping frameworks. No single lens is sufficient on its own.

In Holy Sh*t: A Brief History of Swearing (2013), Mohr proposes that we think about profanity across three intersecting dimensions: physiological, historical/social, and linguistic. She describes the sacred and the obscene as "two spheres of the unsayable" — the things humans across cultures have most consistently marked as off-limits, even as the specific content shifts over time.

Mohr's Framework
Three overlapping lenses for understanding why language becomes profane
Physiological

Profanity has measurable physical effects — on the speaker's body and the listener's nervous system. This lens asks: what does swearing do to us biologically?

Historical / Social

What counts as profane has changed dramatically across time and across cultures. This lens traces how social power shapes which words become taboo and when.

Linguistic

Swear words have phonological patterns, grammatical properties, and semantic structures that distinguish them from other vocabulary. This lens analyses profanity as a linguistic system.

Mohr's historical argument can be summarized simply: at any given moment in English-speaking history, the most powerful profanity has tended to cluster around two poles — the sacred and the scatological/sexual. In medieval England, oaths invoking God's body ("By God's wounds!" "Zounds!") were the most shocking language possible. By the Victorian era, as religion receded from everyday public life, bodily and sexual language moved to the top of the taboo hierarchy. In the 21st century, slurs based on identity have become the most socially consequential category of profanity.

This doesn't mean earlier forms of profanity disappeared. It means the relative weight of different categories shifts as social values shift. Understanding that shift is what Mohr means by the historical lens.

The Shifting History of What's Profane

Antiquity
Divine Invocation
In ancient Greece and Rome, the most powerful taboo language involved invoking the gods inappropriately or cursing enemies through supernatural means. Religious and magical language were deeply intertwined. Curse tablets found throughout the Roman Empire show the power attributed to written imprecations.
May the gods strike you down!
Middle Ages
Sacred Oaths & God's Body
The rise of Judeo-Christian theology made blasphemy and sacrilege the most serious linguistic offences. Swearing by God, Jesus, or the Virgin Mary was both powerful and dangerous. Minced oaths (like "Zounds!" from "God's wounds") emerged as euphemistic workarounds.
Zounds! Gadzooks! By God's Bones!
Renaissance & Enlightenment
Decentering Religion, Centering the Body
As religious authority declined relative to secular social norms, bodily and sexual language began rising in the taboo hierarchy. Concepts of "vulgarity" and class-based disgust became central to policing language. Words that had previously been unremarkable clinical terms became offensive.
Cuckold Knave Scoundrel
Victorian Era
Bodily Repression
Victorian social norms pushed bodily functions and sexuality into intense taboo. Bathroom talk, body parts, and sexual language became primary sites of linguistic regulation. The legacy: most of what we think of today as "classic" English swear words gained their current status in this era.
Arse Cock Piss
20th Century
Sexual Liberation & Rebellion
Sexual liberation movements partially reduced the shock value of sexual language, while the 20th century saw profanity increasingly used as a marker of anti-establishment identity. The rise of mass media created new regulatory regimes. Comedian George Carlin's "Seven Words You Can Never Say on Television" (1972) became a landmark legal case about broadcast profanity in the US.
Fuck Shit Bitch
21st Century
Identity Slurs as the New Sacred
As bodily language becomes more normalized in mainstream media, identity-based slurs — targeting race, gender, sexuality, disability, and other dimensions of personhood — have emerged as the most socially consequential form of profanity. The harm is not just offence but the material consequences for targeted communities.
Slurs targeting race Slurs targeting sexuality Slurs targeting disability

Section 05

Brain, Body & "Kidnappers of Attention"

Steven Pinker calls swear words "kidnappers of attention." This is not just a clever metaphor — it is a description of how these words actually function in the nervous system.

Most language is processed primarily in the cerebral cortex — the large, evolutionarily recent, rational outer layer of the brain. Swear words are different. They have unusually strong connections to the limbic system: the older, deeper brain structures responsible for emotion, fear responses, and automatic reactions. This is why profanity tends to feel more visceral, more immediate, and harder to ignore than ordinary language.

The implications are significant. When you hear a swear word directed at you, your body responds before your rational mind has time to process it. Your heart rate may increase slightly. Skin conductance changes. The "fight or flight" pathways activate, however mildly. This is what Pinker means when he says these words "kidnap" your attention: they bypass deliberate cognitive processing and trigger an automatic, emotionally charged response.

Video: Steven Pinker on Swearing (Big Think)
Steven Pinker explains his "kidnappers of attention" concept and the neuroscience behind why swear words have such disproportionate emotional impact. (~5 min)

Evidence for the limbic processing of profanity comes from several directions. Most strikingly, coprolalia — the involuntary vocalization of obscene or offensive language — is observed in some people with Tourette syndrome. Because the neural pathways involved in producing spontaneous profanity appear to be somewhat distinct from those involved in deliberate speech, damage or disease affecting cortical speech regions can leave swearing capacity intact even when other language functions are severely impaired. Patients with certain forms of aphasia (language loss following stroke or brain injury) sometimes lose the ability to produce controlled speech while retaining the capacity to swear. The poet and physician Michael Corke documented cases in which profanity was among the last coherent speech to survive extensive neural damage.

A Note on Coprolalia

Coprolalia affects only a minority of people with Tourette syndrome — estimates range from 10 to 20 percent. It is important not to equate Tourette's with involuntary swearing; the syndrome encompasses far more. But coprolalia is scientifically significant because it provides a kind of natural experiment: it demonstrates that the neural machinery for producing profanity can function independently of the neural machinery for deliberate, socially controlled speech. What does it mean that the brain has what amounts to a separate, older pathway for the most emotionally loaded words in our vocabulary?

Related to limbic processing is the concept of emotional valence — the positive or negative charge a word carries beyond its literal meaning. The word "shit" literally refers to feces, but its valence can be positive ("that's some good shit"), neutral ("holy shit, look at that!"), or negative ("that's a shit situation"). The same phonological object, same spelling, radically different valence. This is not an accident; it reflects how profanity is stored and processed emotionally rather than purely semantically.

The Stroop effect offers a compelling demonstration of how profane words commandeer cognitive resources. In a standard Stroop test, participants are asked to name the colour of ink a word is printed in, ignoring what the word says. When the word is a swear word, participants are significantly slower and less accurate — the meaning of the word interferes with the simpler perceptual task. Their brains cannot help processing the emotional content of the word even when they are trying not to. The word kidnappers the cognitive process.

Section 06

A Typology of Swearing

Not all profanity is the same. Two different classification systems — one categorizing subject matter, one categorizing function — help us map the full terrain.

The first question to ask about any given swear word or category of profanity is: what domain of human experience does it draw from? The second question is: what is it doing functionally? These are different questions, and the answers don't always overlap in predictable ways.

What Domain Does It Draw From?

Swearing draws from every corner of human culture that a society marks as sensitive, sacred, or shameful. The categories below represent the major thematic clusters — though their relative weight varies significantly across cultures and time periods.

Pinker's Five Types: What Is It Doing?

Steven Pinker argues that profanity can be classified by function — what the speaker is trying to accomplish. The same word can fall into different types depending on context. Click each card to expand. (Pinker, 2007)

▼
Type 1 of 5
Dysphemistic
Making something sound worse. The opposite of euphemism.
Dysphemism uses an offensively blunt substitute for a neutral or positive term. It suggests a negative perception, attitude, or reaction. Calling police "pigs," or referring to someone as a "bastard" rather than their name, frames the subject through a negative lens. Example: "The pigs showed up" instead of "The officers arrived."
▼
Type 2 of 5
Abusive
Directed at people. Designed to hurt, intimidate, or insult.
Abusive swearing is aimed at a person or group, with the goal of threatening, demeaning, or causing emotional harm. This is the category most directly linked to concepts of verbal abuse, harassment, and hate speech. Slurs often function primarily in this register. Example: Any slur deployed to harm or demean; "Fuck you!"
▼
Type 3 of 5
Idiomatic
Figurative, informal. Not literally taboo — used to express enthusiasm, emphasis, or connection.
Idiomatic swearing has largely lost its literal or offensive charge. It functions as a social lubricant, a way of signalling informality, excitement, or camaraderie. Profanity in idioms often reads as unremarkable in casual speech. "Up shit's creek without a paddle" is understood figuratively, not scatologically. Example: "That's some seriously good shit." "I'm so fucked up, man."
▼
Type 4 of 5
Emphatic
Used to show surprise, intensity, or to amplify a statement.
Emphatic profanity functions primarily as an intensifier — it turns up the emotional volume on what is being said. The actual taboo content of the word is secondary to its intensifying function. Political figures and public speakers often deploy this register under pressure. Example: "Get the fuck out of here" (expressing disbelief); "That is absolutely brilliant!"
▼
Type 5 of 5
Cathartic
Involuntary or semi-voluntary. Used for emotional release, often in response to pain or frustration.
Cathartic swearing is often triggered without deliberate intention — the limbic system fires before the cortex has time to intervene (remember Mohr's physiological lens). It provides genuine relief from stress, pain, or frustration. Research by Stephens et al. (2009) found that swearing after pain actually increased pain tolerance — the "hypoalgesic effect." Example: Dropping something on your foot: "SHIT!" Stubbing a toe in the dark.

Section 07

Who Decides?

"Bad words create the boundaries of what we reject and accept as a society." This insight raises an urgent question: who gets to draw those boundaries, and through what mechanisms?

"Bad words create the boundaries of what we reject and accept as a society."
Vsauce (Michael Stevens)

Profanity does not emerge from a neutral process. The words that get coded as taboo, and the severity of their taboo status, reflect the distribution of social power. What is considered grossly offensive in one setting may be entirely acceptable in another — not because people in those settings have different moral sensibilities, but because different institutions, communities, and relationships are operating.

This connects to what Mohr describes as the semantic pejoration process: the way words that refer to marginalized groups tend to become insults over time, regardless of their original meaning. The pattern is consistent: a neutral or even positive descriptor gets applied to a group with low social status, absorbs the negative connotations associated with that group, and eventually becomes an epithet or slur. "Idiot," "moron," and "imbecile" were originally clinical diagnostic terms in early 20th-century psychology. Their transformation into insults reflects how disability has been stigmatized — not anything inherent to the words themselves.

The Layers of Who Decides

Family & Household
The most immediate language socialization. What was allowed or punished in your home shapes your initial sense of what counts as offensive and who has the right to say what.
Community
Peer groups, neighbourhoods, and cultural communities have their own linguistic norms that may differ substantially from mainstream standards. Code-switching — moving between registers — is a daily reality for many speakers.
Social Institutions
Schools, workplaces, churches, and other institutions enforce specific language standards, often backed by formal consequences. These norms reflect institutional power more than any inherent property of the words involved.
Pop Culture & Media
Entertainment, music, and social media both reflect and reshape taboo language. Platforms have their own content moderation policies. What gets broadcast, what gets censored, and what goes viral are all exercises of cultural power.
Government & Law
Broadcasting regulations, obscenity laws, and hate speech legislation formalize some language standards into enforceable rules. Notably, these laws are themselves contested — the line between protected expression and harmful speech is actively debated in every democratic society.
Social Norms / Taboos
The most diffuse but often most powerful layer. Norms are enforced through social pressure, shame, and inclusion/exclusion — often without any explicit rule being stated. These are the "invisible" mechanisms that do most of the work.

Notice that power runs through all of these layers. The family that punishes a child for saying "damn" is not enforcing a natural law; they are transmitting a social norm. The broadcaster that bleeps a slur is making an editorial decision shaped by regulatory history and advertiser pressure. The community that reclaims a word that was previously used as a weapon against it is exercising agency within, and against, these power structures.

Think About It

George Carlin's 1972 "Seven Words You Can Never Say on Television" bit (shown in class) became a Supreme Court case (FCC v. Pacifica Foundation, 1978) that shaped American broadcast law for decades. Carlin's point was not just that censorship is absurd — it was that the arbitrary selection of certain words as unsayable, while allowing discussion of war, murder, and torture, reveals something about whose sensitivities actually get protected. What does your own list of "never say" words reveal about which communities' feelings were prioritized in your upbringing?

Section 08

A Global Lens

Most English-language scholarship on profanity centres Western European and North American contexts. But taboo language is a human universal — and examining non-Western frameworks reveals that the concepts we take for granted are far more culturally specific than they appear.

The frameworks we have covered so far — Mohr's three lenses, Pinker's types, the use-mention distinction — are powerful analytical tools, but they emerge from a particular intellectual tradition. They were developed primarily by English-speaking scholars working within Western academic institutions, drawing mostly on English-language data. That is worth naming.

Non-Western frameworks for understanding language power are not supplementary context. They are analytical frameworks in their own right — and in some cases they describe aspects of the relationship between language and harm that Western models have historically undertheorized.

Japanese Tradition
Kotodama (言霊)
The belief that words carry inherent spiritual power — that language is not merely referential but efficacious. Speaking ill is not just rude; it risks actually bringing that ill into being. This framework provides a culturally grounded account of why "words are not just words" — a debate Western philosophy approaches differently through speech act theory.
Yoruba Tradition
Egan (derogatory speech)
In Yoruba oral traditions, certain categories of derogatory speech carry obligations of social repair. Language that damages someone's social standing creates responsibilities for the speaker that extend beyond simple apology. This embeds linguistic ethics into a framework of social relationship and community accountability.
Indigenous North American
Relational Language Philosophy
Many Indigenous language traditions understand language as fundamentally relational — words do not simply label objects or feelings; they enact relationships. Language that disrupts relational harmony is understood as harmful on those grounds, not simply because it violates an abstract norm of decency.
Arabic & Islamic Tradition
Linguistic Ethics (Adab al-kalam)
Classical Islamic scholarship developed extensive frameworks for evaluating speech ethically, distinguishing categories of harmful, offensive, and permissible language with considerable nuance. These traditions predate and parallel many Western academic frameworks, and address questions about harm and intent that contemporary linguistics is only recently theorizing rigorously.
Post-Colonial Framework
Ngugi wa Thiong'o
The Kenyan writer Ngugi wa Thiong'o argues that colonial imposition of European languages was itself a form of linguistic violence — one that restructured which words, concepts, and entire ways of organizing experience were available for expression. This means that questions of "profanity" in post-colonial contexts cannot be separated from questions of linguistic power and cultural survival.
Cross-Cultural Pattern
Universal but Not Uniform
Despite the enormous variation in what counts as taboo across cultures, there are striking patterns: virtually all human societies have categories of sacred speech, of speech that carries dangerous power, and of speech that violates relationships or communal norms. Profanity appears to be a human universal — but its specific content is always culturally determined.
Why This Matters for Your Analysis

If you analyze a word that primarily affects a community outside the Western European tradition — a slur targeting an Indigenous group, a blasphemy from a non-Christian tradition, a word whose taboo status is rooted in a non-Western cultural framework — using only Pinker's or Mohr's frameworks will give you an incomplete analysis. The frameworks in this section can help you think about harm, power, and taboo in ways that are more responsive to the specific cultural contexts involved.

Wrap-Up

Putting It Together

Module 1 has given you a set of analytical tools rather than a single definition. That is intentional. You should now be able to:

  1. Explain why profanity cannot be adequately defined by any single framework — and what each of the five major definition types captures and misses
  2. Use key terminology (profanity, swearing, slur, blasphemy, euphemism, dysphemism, taboo) precisely
  3. Apply Mohr's three lenses (physiological, historical/social, linguistic) to analyze why a given word or category of language is considered profane

  6. Identify the social forces and power structures that shape which language gets designated as taboo
  7. Recognize the limits of Western-centric frameworks and apply at least one non-Western analytical lens
